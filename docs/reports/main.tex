\section{Introduction}
Chuck-a-Luck, also known as Bird Cage, is a game where players bet on a number from 1 to 6. Three fair dice are rolled, and the player is paid based on the number of matching dice. Standard payouts are usually 1:1 for a single match, 2:1 for two matches, and 3:1 for three matches. If no dice match the chosen number, the wager is lost. This game is used to demonstrate fundamental concepts in probability and statistics. This report analyzes a simulation engine developed in R Shiny. The primary objectives are to verify the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) and evaluate Frequentist Confidence Intervals in quantifying uncertainty for winning probabilities.

\section{Theoretical Foundations}
Chuck-a-Luck is a binomial experiment where a player bets on a number $b \in \{1, 2, ..., 6\}$. Three fair dice are rolled, and the number of matches $X$ follows a Binomial distribution $B(3, 1/6)$. The sample space contains 216 combinations, summarized in Table \ref{tab:outcomes}.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \caption{Chuck-a-Luck Theoretical Outcome Distribution}
    \begin{tabular}{llcc}
        \toprule
        Outcome (Matches) & Combinations & Prob. (Ratio) & Prob. (\%) \\
        \midrule
        0 Matches (Loss) & $125/216$ & 57.87\% \\
        1 Match          & $75/216$  & 34.72\% \\
        2 Matches         & $15/216$  & 6.94\%  \\
        3 Matches         & $1/216$   & 0.46\%  \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \label{tab:outcomes}
\end{table}

The House Edge represents the operator's mathematical advantage, calculated as the negative of the Expected Value $E[X]$ for a \$1 wager:
\begin{equation}
E[X] = \sum_{i=0}^{3} P(X=i) \cdot \text{Payout}_i
\end{equation}
Using standard payouts ($\{-1, 1, 2, 3\}$), we have:
\begin{equation*}
E[X] = \frac{125}{216}(-1) + \frac{75}{216}(1) + \frac{15}{216}(2) + \frac{1}{216}(3) = -\frac{17}{216} \approx -7.87\%
\end{equation*}
This means that for every \$100 wagered, a player is statistically expected to lose \$7.87.

\section{Architecture and Implementation}
The simulator is implemented using the R programming language \cite{rproject} and the Shiny web framework \cite{shiny}.

\subsection{Backend Logic and Optimization}
To support large-scale simulations, we utilize a vectorized backend. Successes for each trial are calculated using row-weighted sums: \texttt{Matches <- rowSums(dice == target)}. This optimization enables sub-second execution even for millions of trials. Additionally, simulation state is persisted across multiple runs using \texttt{reactiveValues}, allowing users to cumulatively build a large dataset without reloading the session.

\subsection{Frequentist Confidence Intervals}
To quantify uncertainty for the estimate $\hat{p}$, we implement Wald, Wilson Score, and Agresti-Coull intervals. Let $z$ be the $1 - \alpha/2$ quantile of the standard normal distribution:

\begin{equation}
\text{Wald:} \quad \hat{p} \pm z \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\text{Wilson Score:} \quad \frac{\hat{p} + \frac{z^2}{2n} \pm z \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n} + \frac{z^2}{4n^2}}}{1 + \frac{z^2}{n}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\text{Agresti-Coull:} \quad \tilde{n} = n + z^2, \quad \tilde{p} = \frac{X + z^2/2}{\tilde{n}}, \quad \tilde{p} \pm z \sqrt{\frac{\tilde{p}(1-\tilde{p})}{\tilde{n}}}
\end{equation}

Figure \ref{fig:ci_suite} presents an integrated view of these three methodologies as implemented in the simulator's analysis dashboard.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{ci_analysis_suite.png}
    \caption{Integrated Confidence Analysis dashboard, showcasing interval methodologies, numerical summaries, and $1/\sqrt{n}$ width shrinkage behavior.}
    \label{fig:ci_suite}
\end{figure}

Analytical results demonstrate that for rare outcomes at small sample sizes, both the Wald and Agresti-Coull intervals can yield invalid limits below 0, whereas the Wilson Score method remains robustly and naturally bounded within the probability space. As $n$ increases, all three methodologies converge as the binomial distribution approaches normality.
